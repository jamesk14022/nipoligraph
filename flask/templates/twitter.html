What They Say

Most of the NI MLAs and MPs are active on Twitter, so tracking this activity can provide some insight into their political outlook, and their engagement with the public and their colleagues. Tweets are collected weekly, starting from July 2020.

Top tweeters

These are the most active politicians on Twitter, by total tweets in the past month.

Most impactful tweeters

This plot shows the politicians with the biggest impact on Twitter, as measured by the average number of retweets received by each of their original tweets in the past month (measured after around one week, so an underestimate of the true retweet rate).

Retweet networks

This network plot shows how 'connected' the politicians are to one another, measured through Twitter retweets, since 1 July 2020. Connections are drawn in both directions (giving and receiving retweets), but you can hover on a politician to show only the connections for retweets that they received from others. See Technical Stuff for more explanation.
Sinn Fein members form a much more active tweet-and-retweet network than do DUP members - many of the latter's older MLAs rarely or never use Twitter.

Most positive, negative tweeters

This plot shows the politicians whose tweets (including retweets) scored highest and lowest, on average, for positive sentiment (calculated as the VADER 'compound' score), in the past month.

Technical Stuff

  • Twitter data
  • Tweet content map
  • Network plot
Tweets are collected from Twitter once a week using the R package rtweet. The number of retweets for each tweet is recorded at this time, so some tweets will have had a full week to collect retweets, while others will have had only a day or less. The retweet rates should therefore be taken as the average number of retweets obtained in the first 3-4 days of a tweet's lifetime.
The tweet content plot uses, first, word embeddings (Word2Vec, with 50 dimensions, trained on 180,000 tweets), and then Principal Component Analysis, to reduce the content of each tweet into two numerical dimensions. These co-ordinates are averaged for each tweeter. Tweeters that are positioned close together on the plot tend to use similar words in their tweets. Note however that, as can be expected, reducing the complexity of hundreds of tweets into two numbers is a big over-simplification, as indicated on the axis labels: the position of the politicians on these two axes only explains 25% of the variance, or differences, in the content of their tweets.
Point positions for the tweet network plot were calculated with the R package ggnet2. Each tweeter is positioned near to those that they frequently retweet or are retweeted by, and the thickness of the edge (line) connecting two tweeters is proportional to the number of times they retweeted one another (whichever is the larger of the two directions), in the past month. The size of each node (circle) is proportional to the total number of retweets received. Note that due to the weekly data collection frequency, these are underestimates of the true, 'final', retweet numbers.
Hovering over a node highlights the incoming edges, which are the retweets received by that politician. The most influential politicians - those who are frequently retweeted - tend to appear near the centre of a cluster, with large nodes and many connecting edges. Politicians positioned near the middle of the plot are those that get some retweets from outside their party.

Tweet content

This plot positions tweeters relative to one another based on the content of their tweets: those close together tend to tweet 'about the same things', in a simplified sense.
